About this Graph

The candlestick chart originated with the Japanese. It was invented by farmers to keep track of the market value of merchandise such as rice. It currently is used in the same capacity -showing the price of stocks and other such items. Each candlestick has an opening, closing, high, and low value. There is one candlestick per time interval. With the one candlestick, you are able to see what the price of the item started out at, what it ended at and what the peak high and low values were. Often, the candlestick is colored based on whether the closing price was higher or lower than the opening price. This helps people see at a glance the main information, as well as give them the opportunity to look at the details. Here, the green color represents the closing price was higher than the opening price, while red represents the opposite. There is no legend here as this is a fairly standard coloring scheme. While this visual is highly effective for showing information such as you would get from market values, it does not expand well to other types of information.

Marks and Channels

Marks

Marks include two sets of lines. The first line shows the high and low values. The second line shows the opening and closing values. In both cases, the width of the line is irrelevant as it is not changed by the data; therefore, the mark is the line and not the area.

Channels

Channels include the position of the lines on a common scale. There is the vertical point for the line based on the opening value, the vertical point for the line based on the low value, the vertical point for the ending of the line based on closing value, and the vertical point for the line based on the high value. Compared to the bar graph, where the length of the line is the chalnnel, here the points are the channel. This is because while the lines in bar graphs all start at 0 on the y-axis, in this visualization, the starting point as well as the ending point can change. Another channel may be the color of the lines. Often, this channel is used to identify whether the item's opening value was higher or lower than the closing value.
